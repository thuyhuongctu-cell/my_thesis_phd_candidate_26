## Signature sentence patterns

### 1. The "analytically valuable setting" opener
Every paper opens with a context-justification sentence that explains *why this country/setting creates
inferential leverage* — never just stating that the topic is important.

> "Vietnam offers an analytically valuable setting for revisiting the internationalisation–performance (I–P) relationship because firms expand abroad under conditions of institutional transition, uneven capability accumulation, and rapidly changing digital infrastructure."

> "Singapore is an analytically valuable setting for revisiting the internationalization–performance relationship because it combines high digital maturity with firm-level heterogeneity in export intensity."

**Pattern**: `[Country] offers an analytically valuable setting for [revisiting X] because [2–3 specific institutional conditions that enable inference].`

---

### 2. The "two opposing forces" structure
Theory sections habitually pair the upside and downside mechanisms as two explicitly named forces before
the net prediction is stated.

> "Two opposing forces underpin the curvature. On the upside, increasing direct-export intensity creates scale economies, knowledge spillovers from foreign customers, and learning-by-exporting effects that lift productivity. On the downside, coordinating production for institutionally distant markets imposes information-processing costs that grow nonlinearly..."

**Pattern**: `Two [opposing / distinct / parallel] [forces / mechanisms / considerations] [underpin / operate through / shape] [X]. On the [upside/one side], [mechanism A]. On the [downside/other side], [mechanism B].`

---

### 3. The mechanism-naming sentence
Mechanisms are never described generically. They are given a named theoretical anchor immediately
followed by a cited reference.

> "The mechanism is institutional transaction costs (Williamson, 1985; Hennart, 2007): in a transitional economy, enforcement gaps, logistics deficits, and information asymmetries raise the marginal cost of managing each additional foreign market..."

**Pattern**: `The mechanism is [named theoretical concept] ([Author, Year; Author, Year]): [specific causal description of how X produces Y].`

---

### 4. The "three gaps motivate this study" structure
Research gap sections open with a direct count and then parse each gap by number. The gaps are specific
lacunae, not "more research is needed" gestures.

> "Three gaps motivate this study. First, existing work often treats digitalisation as a broadly positive resource without paying sufficient attention to temporal and contextual variation in its payoff. [...] Second, the distinction between technological capability and digital adoption remains underdeveloped. [...] Third, pooled estimates may obscure substantial lifecycle heterogeneity."

**Pattern**: `[N] gaps motivate this study. First, [specific gap — what prior work conflates or omits]. Second, [construct distinction gap]. Third, [temporal/methodological gap].`

---

### 5. The "structural durability vs. environmental shift" competing-interpretation frame
When comparing findings across time or contexts, the author names the two competing interpretations
explicitly before the evidence decides between them. Labels are paired with hypothesis numbers.

> "Environmental-shift arguments (Hitt et al., 1997; Xiao et al., 2013) predict that the inverted-U threshold's position and curvature will be sensitive to structural context, generating a 'wave-specific' pattern (H2a). Structural-durability arguments (Bausch and Krist, 2007; Marano et al., 2016) suggest that the β₁ (FSTS) and β₂ (FSTS²) coefficients will remain statistically indistinguishable across waves (H2b)."

---

### 6. Bold-phrase summary sentences for headline findings
Key empirical claims are bolded inline when they represent the headline take-away, especially in
introductions and highlights sections.

> "**The inverted-U is fundamentally a step function across the export-participation barrier rather than a continuous within-exporter saturation curve**"

---

### 7. Informative-null reframing
When a test does not reject a null, the non-rejection is reframed as a theoretically informative signal
rather than treated as a failure or absence of evidence.

> "This is an informative positive null — consistent with the saturation hypothesis — rather than an anomaly."

> "The Lind–Mehlum p = .303 is therefore a *positive theoretical signal* under the saturation framework, not a methodological failure."

---

### 8. Boundary-condition caveats embedded within argument paragraphs
Scope limitations are stated within the paragraph making the claim, not deferred to a limitations section.

> "This within-context evidence from Singapore supports a contingent digital complementarity mechanism... The study therefore qualifies rather than overturns the nonlinear I–P literature."

> "**WBES-scope caveat.** Because the WBES Vietnam instrument carries only the website-presence binary (c22b) comparably across the 2009 / 2015 / 2023 waves, this paper does not attempt to test higher-tier digital-transformation mechanisms..."

## Theory-evidence linking formulas

| Formula | Example from manuscripts |
|---------|-------------------------|
| `consistent with [X] predictions` | "consistent with the inverted-U specification" / "consistent with Uppsala stage theory" |
| `is most consistent with [interpretation]` | "The negative linear coefficient is most consistent with an early-stage cost-dominance reading of H1." |
| `the evidence points toward [Y]` | Used in Discussion openings when reconciling prior expectation with actual result |
| `diverges from [X] in [specific dimension]` | "aligns with Hitt et al. (1997) in direction but diverges in turning-point location" |
| `[finding] is theoretically interpretable: [mechanism]` | "This divergence is theoretically interpretable: institutional voids in emerging markets extend the early-stage cost-dominance zone..." |
| `Taken together, these patterns are most consistent with [interpretation]` | Standard closing phrase for empirical paragraphs |
| `This pattern suggests...` | For descriptive, non-causal readings |
| `the [Lind–Mehlum / Paternoster] test [confirms / does not reject]` | "Paternoster z-tests do not reject coefficient equality across waves (FSTS z = +0.82, p = .412)" |
| `Against the prior expectation of [X], the evidence is consistent with [Y].` | Standard Discussion opening |
| `The near-flat pattern observed in [specification]... instead indicates that...` | Redirecting evidence from one mechanism to a different reading |
| `we interpret this as` | "the negative DAI × FSTS interaction observed only in 2023 [...] is read as Tier-1 proxy obsolescence" |
| `this pattern is consistent with H[n], though the coefficient magnitude suggests [qualification]` | When confirming a hypothesis with a nuance |

**Core rule**: Never "supports H[n]" alone. Always: report coefficient + SE + p-value; contextualize using
descriptive statistics; connect to the named theoretical mechanism.

## Hedging vocabulary

Arranged from weakest to strongest assertion:

### Strongest hedges (uncertain / exploratory claims)
- "is consistent with"
- "provides preliminary evidence for"
- "is most plausibly read as"
- "may generate" / "may vary" / "may shift"
- "is plausibly the dominant..."
- "we treat this as..." (distancing language for contested interpretations)
- "cannot be ruled out"
- "an empirical question that this paper treats as..."

### Moderate hedges (well-supported, associational claims)
- "suggests"
- "indicates"
- "appears to"
- "is associated with" (preferred over "affects" in cross-sectional OLS)
- "points toward"
- "is informative"
- "the evidence is consistent with"

### Light hedges (robust, replicable, formally tested findings)
- "demonstrates"
- "reveals"
- "documents"
- "the analysis shows that" (only when the claim is tightly bounded)

### Forbidden hedging language (never used in these manuscripts)
- "proves"
- "confirms"
- "shows that [causal claim]" without instrumental identification
- "marginally significant" — explicitly banned
- "almost significant"
- "highly significant"

## Transition preferences

### Substantive connectives (actually used across P3/P4/P5)
- "Against this baseline expectation, [finding/theory predicts otherwise]..."
- "A second and distinct mechanism operates through [pathway]..."
- "Compounding this effect, [related mechanism] predicts..."
- "Taken together, these patterns are most consistent with [interpretation]..."
- "This argument is especially plausible in [context] because..."
- "Two further considerations sharpen the prediction."
- "A second consideration arises from the structure of..."
- "This expectation is particularly relevant in [setting], where..."
- "Read together, [A] and [B] imply..."
- "Consistent with this logic:"
- "The mechanism is [named concept] ([citations]):"
- "This framing matters for theoretical positioning..."
- "This is not a contradiction of [prior work] but a specification of [boundary condition]..."
- "Building on this, [next prediction]..."

### Avoid generic connectives
| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "In addition, ..." | "A second and distinct mechanism operates through [pathway]..." |
| "Furthermore, ..." | "Compounding this effect, [related mechanism] predicts..." |
| "However, ..." | "Against this baseline expectation, [specific finding/theory]..." |
| "Therefore, ..." | "Taken together, these patterns are most consistent with..." |
| "This supports our hypothesis." | "This pattern is consistent with H[n], though the coefficient magnitude suggests [qualification]..." |
| "In conclusion, ..." | "The convergence of [finding 1] and [finding 2] implies [theoretical advance]..." |
| "Many scholars argue..." | "[Author1 et al., Year] and [Author2, Year] both argue, from different theoretical traditions, that..." |
| "It can be seen that..." | "[Figure N / Table N] reveals [specific pattern]: [β] at [level A] versus [β] at [level B]..." |
| "This shows..." | "The evidence is consistent with..." or "This pattern suggests..." |
| "X is important because..." | "X operates as [moderator/boundary condition] because [mechanism]..." |

## Citation integration style

### Two modes: loud (inline) vs. soft (parenthetical)

**Loud references** — when directly engaging a scholar's argument:
> "Hitt et al. (1997) introduced the inverted-U logic: initial internationalization yields scale and learning benefits..."
> "Lind and Mehlum (2010) criteria — significant positive slope at the left bound and significant negative slope at the right bound — are verified for each wave."

**Soft references** — for evidence clusters or foundational anchors:
> "...has been documented across emerging-market contexts (Marano et al., 2016; Hennart, 2011; Contractor, 2007)."
> "...drawing on the absorptive-capacity logic of Cohen and Levinthal (1990) and the technological-capability tradition of Lall (1992)."

### Citation conventions observed
- Multiple parenthetical sources: alphabetical order by first author surname
- APA 7th throughout; no footnotes
- High-density citation clusters appear in literature-review paragraphs; mechanism-explanation sentences carry one or two anchor citations
- Theory anchors typically pair the founding paper with a recent application or replication:
  - "Lall (1992) ... and Cohen and Levinthal (1990)"
  - "Bharadwaj et al. (2013); Verhoef et al. (2021)" — for the digital-adoption tier hierarchy
  - "Johanson and Vahlne (1977); Vahlne and Johanson (2017)" — for Uppsala model evolution
- Parenthetical citations in Findings sections are sparse; the emphasis is on the β / SE / p-value triad

## AVOID these patterns

### 1. "Marginally significant" (and variants)
Never used. Report the exact p-value and explain the inference explicitly.
- **Wrong**: "The coefficient is marginally significant (p = .08)"
- **Right**: "The coefficient falls below the .05 threshold (β = 0.12, SE = 0.07, p = .086); we do not interpret this as evidence for the hypothesis."

### 2. "Supports H[n]" without mechanism
- **Wrong**: "β = +1.28, p = .001, supporting H1."
- **Right**: "The positive FSTS coefficient (β = +1.28, SE = 0.379, p = .001) is consistent with the learning-by-exporting prediction of H1: at mean export intensity (5.2 %), firms in the ascending portion of the I–P curve realize scale and knowledge gains before the coordination-cost threshold binds."

### 3. Overclaiming causal language in OLS cross-sections
Causal verbs ("increases," "decreases," "causes") describe theoretical mechanisms only, never OLS results.
- **Wrong**: "Higher TCI causes better performance."
- **Right**: "TCI is positively associated with productivity (β = 0.179, p < .001), consistent with the absorptive-capacity channel."

### 4. Single-mechanism explanations without a named theoretical pathway
- **Wrong**: "Experience helps businesses overcome barriers in international markets."
- **Right**: "Accumulated managerial experience reduces psychic distance (Johanson & Vahlne, 1977) by compressing the information-processing demand of cross-border coordination."

### 5. Generic future directions
- **Wrong**: "More research is needed to examine other factors."
- **Right**: "A natural experiment exploiting [specific policy change] as an instrument for [endogenous variable] would resolve the selection concern that constrains OLS interpretation here. Two extensions would sharpen causal identification. First, [specific mechanism + instrument]. Second, [specific construct distinction]."

### 6. Bullet-list limitations
Limitations are integrated as prose "inferential bounds," each structured as: (a) what cannot be
concluded, (b) why the existing robustness check is insufficient, (c) what specific method would resolve it.
- **Wrong**: "Limitations: (1) cross-sectional; (2) single country; (3) single-item measures."
- **Right**: "First, the cross-sectional WBES structure precludes causal identification... Future work with [instrument] would resolve this..."

### 7. "Interesting" / "significant" as free-floating adjectives
- **Wrong**: "This is an interesting finding."
- **Right**: Report *what is notable quantitatively* — direction vs. prior expectation, magnitude relative to mean, turning-point location relative to meta-analytic baseline.

### 8. Contractions
Never used. "don't" to "do not"; "can't" to "cannot"; "it's" to "it is"; "we've" to "we have."

### 9. Topic-first (generic) opening sentences
- **Wrong**: "Internationalization is an important strategy for firm performance..."
- **Right**: "[Country] offers an analytically valuable setting for revisiting [relationship] because [specific institutional conditions that enable inference]."

### 10. Citation as confirmation stamp only
Never "This is consistent with Smith (2020) who found X" as the full sentence. Always add: direction of
agreement or divergence + specific dimension + theoretical explanation for any divergence.

### 11. Asterisk significance in prose
Asterisks belong in tables only. In running text: "p = .003", not "p < .05*" or "statistically
significant***."

### 12. "Digital capability" label when the measure is Tier-1 only
A single website-presence binary is labeled "foundational digital adoption" or "digital presence," not
"digital capability" — the author is explicit about construct-label calibration to actual item content.

## Voice checklist for new sections

Use before submitting any drafted section for review:

1. **Opening sentence test**: Does it justify *why this context creates inferential leverage*, not just assert the topic is important? (Template: "[Country/context] offers an analytically valuable setting for [revisiting X] because [2–3 specific institutional/structural conditions that enable inference].")

2. **Mechanism completeness test**: Are all theoretical pathways named with a concept label and ≥1 citation? ("The mechanism is [named concept] ([Author, Year]):...") Vague language like "helps" or "overcomes barriers" signals a missing pathway.

3. **Hedging calibration test**: Are OLS results in associational language ("is associated with," "is consistent with") not causal language ("causes," "proves," "confirms")? Is "marginally significant" absent? Is every p-value exact?

4. **Coefficient reporting completeness**: Does every β appear with SE (or CI) and exact p-value? Standard format: "β = −0.267 (SE = 0.041, p < .001)" or "β = −0.267 (SE = 0.041, p < .001, 95% CI [−0.348, −0.186])."

5. **H[n] mechanism test**: Does every hypothesis reference name a mechanism and include descriptive contextualization — not just a p-value verdict? "Consistent with H1" is complete only when followed by the mechanism.

6. **Transition substantiveness test**: Are paragraph connectives naming the logical relationship (e.g., "Against this baseline expectation," "Compounding this effect," "Taken together") rather than generic connectives ("Furthermore," "Moreover," "In addition")?

7. **Citation style test**: Loud references (Author, Year, verb) for direct engagement; parenthetical clusters for background evidence. APA 7th throughout. No asterisks in prose.
