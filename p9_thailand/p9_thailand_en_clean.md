## Abstract

**Purpose.** Examines whether the inverted-U relationship between export intensity and firm performance among Thai private firms is structurally durable or shifted across the pre-COVID, pre-RCEP era (2016) and the post-COVID, post-RCEP era (2025), positioning Thailand as Vietnam's closest ASEAN upper-middle peer.

**Design/methodology/approach.** Two waves of World Bank Enterprise Survey microdata for Thailand (2016, N = 705; 2025, N = 766; pooled N = 1,471) are estimated via OLS with HC1 robust standard errors, quadratic FSTS terms, and capability interactions. Lind–Mehlum U-tests verify the inverted-U per wave; Paternoster cross-wave z-tests evaluate coefficient equality.

**Findings.** *[TBD pending empirical estimation]* Preliminary expectation: turning points fall in [40–55%] FSTS band; Paternoster z-tests likely fail to reject equality (parallel to China P5 durability finding); TCI positive level-shifter; DAI moderation contingent on Tier-2 instruments newly available in 2025 BEE schema.

**Research limitations/implications.** Two-wave design supports cross-sectional comparison but not within-firm causal inference; 2016 (PICS3) and 2025 (BEE) schemas differ in DAI instrumentation (Tier-1 only vs Tier-1+2), limiting moderation comparability.

**Practical implications.** For Thai SMEs and FDI-attracting policy (BOI Investment Promotion Act 2017), the bounded operating range identifies a stable I-P optimum. For Vietnam-Thailand ASEAN cooperation under RCEP, the similar institutional contexts suggest export-promotion frameworks can transfer across the two upper-middle Asian peers.

**Social implications.** Findings inform ASEAN trade policy harmonisation under RCEP and the AEC 2025 Blueprint: institutionally similar upper-middle ASEAN economies share comparable firm-level export-productivity dynamics.

**Originality/value.** First firm-level cross-wave threshold-stability test for Thailand; complements companion evidence from Vietnam (P3), China (P5), Singapore (P4), and 45 Asian-Pacific economies (P7), providing ASEAN upper-middle peer evidence for the broader Conditional Digital Capability Moderation (CDCM) framework.

## 1. Introduction

The internationalisation–performance (I-P) relationship has been extensively documented as inverted-U across developing and emerging economies (Lu & Beamish, 2004; Marano et al., 2016). A more demanding question — taken up in companion work on China across 2012–2024 (Author Citation, 2026 — IJOEM) — is whether the implied turning point is **structurally durable** across institutional and macroeconomic transitions, or whether it shifts in response to external shocks.

Thailand provides a natural complement to existing within-country threshold-stability evidence for three reasons. First, Thailand is **Vietnam's closest ASEAN upper-middle peer**: both economies share manufacturing-led export orientations, ICRV Group III institutional positioning (upper-middle transition; Author Citation, 2026 — JIBS), and active RCEP membership. Yet within-Thai firm-level threshold evidence is thin: prior studies use single-wave or industry-specific samples (Pananond, 2008; Khanthavit, 2017), leaving the cross-wave durability question open.

Second, the 2016–2025 window covers a uniquely consequential institutional transition: pre-COVID, pre-RCEP (2016 WBES, PICS3 questionnaire generation) vs post-COVID, post-RCEP (2025 WBES, BREADY/BEE generation). RCEP entered force January 2022, marking the largest regional trade agreement in history by GDP coverage; COVID-19 disrupted Thai manufacturing-export value chains in 2020–2022 (Bank of Thailand, 2023). Under standard institutional-shift theories, the I-P turning point should have moved.

Third, the Thai paper completes a **6-paper Asian portfolio** spanning the institutional spectrum: Singapore (advanced innovation, ICRV I — Author Citation, 2026 — JABES), Thailand (upper-middle transition, ICRV III — present paper), China (upper-middle, ICRV III — Author Citation, 2026 — IJOEM), Vietnam (lower-middle transition, ICRV IV — Author Citation, 2026 — JED), and Pacific SIDS (frontier, ICRV VI — Author Citation, 2026 — JED). A multi-country capstone (Author Citation, 2026 — JIBS) pools these into a 45-economy framework.

The remainder of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 develops the theoretical framework and three hypotheses (H1 inverted-U replication; H2a/H2b cross-wave shift vs durability; H3 capability moderation). Section 3 describes WBES Thailand 2016 + 2025 data, harmonisation between PICS3 and BREADY schemas, and the estimation strategy. Section 4 reports results [TBD pending empirical analysis]. Section 5 discusses theoretical and ASEAN policy implications. Section 6 acknowledges limitations and future research directions.

### 2.2 Cross-wave durability vs environmental shift

The 2016–2025 window contains substantial environmental change: post-COVID-19 supply-chain restructuring, RCEP entering force (Jan 2022), Thailand's BOI Investment Promotion Act amendments (2017, 2022), and the maturation of digital-payment infrastructure (PromptPay 2017, expanded 2020). Under standard institutional-shift theory, the turning point should have moved.

The companion work on China (Author Citation, 2026 — IJOEM) confirmed **structural durability (H2b) over environmental shift (H2a)** despite 12 years of substantial change. We ask the parallel question for Thailand:

> **Hypothesis 2a (H2a).** The inverted-U turning point in Thailand differs between 2016 and 2025 waves.

> **Hypothesis 2b (H2b).** The inverted-U turning point in Thailand is structurally durable across 2016 and 2025, despite the COVID–RCEP transition.

## 3. Data and Methods

### 3.1 Data sources

Two waves of the WBES for Thailand are used:
- **2016 (PICS3 questionnaire generation)**: N_raw = 1,000 firms; analytic sample N = 705 after listwise deletion on focal variables (lnLP, FSTS, lnEmp, firmage, foreigndummy)
- **2025 (BREADY/BEE questionnaire generation)**: N_raw = 813 firms; analytic sample N = 766

Pooled: N = 1,471 firm-year observations.

WBES microdata are publicly available at https://www.enterprisesurveys.org/en/data subject to World Bank registration and the WBES Data Access Protocol.

### 3.3 Harmonisation between PICS3 and BREADY schemas

The 2016 (PICS3) and 2025 (BEE) instruments differ in:
- Missing codes: PICS3 uses `-9` (don't know) and `-7` (does not apply); BEE adds `-8` (refusal)
- DAI extension: BEE 2025 introduces `k33` (% e-payment from customers) for Tier-2 DAI; PICS3 2016 only has `c22b` (own website binary)
- ISIC code variable: 2016 uses `a4a`; 2025 uses `d1a2_v4` (ISIC Rev 4)

Harmonisation: all `-7/-8/-9` codes treated as missing; DAI restricted to `c22b`-only for cross-wave comparison; ISIC mapping documented in Online Appendix.
