Dear Editor,

We are pleased to submit our manuscript, **"The Export Intensity–Performance Relationship in Chinese Private Firms: A Threshold-Stability Perspective,"** for consideration by the *Asia Pacific Journal of Management*. The paper develops and tests a directional prediction about the temporal evolution of the inverted-U relationship between export intensity and firm performance among Chinese private firms, using two waves of the World Bank Enterprise Survey (WBES) for China — 2012 (N = 2,619) and 2024 (N = 1,940; pooled N = 4,559) — separated by more than a decade of substantial structural change in the Chinese economy.

## Why APJM

APJM is the natural home for this paper for three reasons. First, the journal's editorial focus on management research grounded in the Asia Pacific context aligns directly with our country-specific empirical setting (China) and our broader research stream on emerging-Asia firm-performance heterogeneity. Second, APJM has been a key outlet for cross-temporal and cross-country tests of nonlinear strategy–performance relationships in the region, including studies in the inverted-U / threshold tradition our paper extends. Third, the paper's methodological emphasis — Paternoster (1998) z-tests and joint F-tests for cross-wave coefficient equality, complemented by Lind & Mehlum (2010) U-tests and delta-method turning-point confidence intervals — is consistent with the methodological standards APJM has historically signalled.

## Contribution

The paper makes four contributions to the international-business and strategic-management literatures:

1. **Threshold-stability evidence within a single national context.** Prior work has established the inverted-U as a robust pattern in cross-sectional and meta-analytic settings, but rarely tested whether the implied turning point is stable across distinct survey waves within one country. We test that question directly and document an unexpected stability finding: despite multiple structural-change mechanisms predicting a shift, the turning point remains in a remarkably tight range (49.4 % in 2012, 47.2 % in 2024, 48.8 % pooled) with overlapping 95 % confidence intervals. This is a *predicted-shift, observed-stability* result — a substantively informative null against a strong directional prior, recasting the inverted-U threshold from a sample-specific regularity into an enduring structural feature of the setting.

2. **Capability as level-shifter rather than curvature moderator.** The paper distinguishes capability's two conjectured roles: raising productivity uniformly across export intensity (level shift) versus relaxing the structural overextension constraint at the upper tail (curvature moderation). The data robustly support the level-shift role for technological capability (TCI z-coefficients +0.26 to +0.43, p < .001 across waves) but provide only marginal support for curvature moderation (joint F = 3.26, p = .039) and clearly null evidence for capability-conditioned dynamic moderation (F = 0.27, p = .760). This refines the absorptive-capacity tradition (Lall, 1992; Cohen & Levinthal, 1990) by clarifying which performance margin capability affects.

3. **Convergent null evidence on three predicted moderation channels.** Three independent moderation channels — H2 cross-wave shift, H3 working-capital conditioning of the post-threshold downturn, and H4 capability curvature moderation — all return null or unstable evidence. We argue that the convergence is itself substantively meaningful: it supports an interpretation in which the Chinese internationalization–performance trade-off is *durably structural* rather than wave-specific, capability-conditioned, or financial-friction-conditioned.

4. **Methodological triangulation for nonlinear-stability testing.** We demonstrate the value of combining (a) wave-by-wave estimates with (b) Paternoster z-tests on individual coefficients and (c) joint F-tests in a pooled three-way specification. The triangulation yields a more robust verdict than any single test alone, particularly important when the substantive claim hinges on a *failure to reject* a null. The protocol can be adopted by future studies of temporal stability in nonlinear strategy–performance relationships.

## Methodological positioning

The empirical strategy is OLS with Huber–White (HC1) robust standard errors; the pooled specification additionally clusters on the firm identifier `idstd` to address dependence introduced by 217 panel firms re-interviewed across waves. Sample-selection diagnostics include (a) cluster-robust SE on idstd, (b) re-estimation excluding the 217 panel firms (a 1.9 percentage-point shift in the turning point that remains within the 95 % delta-method CI), and (c) a Heckman-style probit with sampling region as the exclusion restriction (the inverse Mills ratio is significant in 2012 only, with a sub-0.10 effect on the FSTS / FSTS² coefficients when included). A manufacturing-only robustness check (§4.6) yields a directionally consistent threshold-stability finding with reduced power. Collectively, these diagnostics support reading the OLS estimates as well-identified associations within the survey-design assumptions.

## Originality and prior submission

This manuscript is original work and is not currently under review at any other journal. Earlier conference and proceedings work by the authors on the broader internationalization–performance question (cited in the manuscript as Do & Tu, 2025; Do & Tu, 2026) addresses adjacent questions — meta-analytic effect-size synthesis and country-cluster heterogeneity respectively — and does not overlap with the within-country threshold-stability test that is the specific contribution of the present paper.
