**Submission of Manuscript: "The Export Intensity–Performance Relationship in Chinese Private Firms: A Threshold-Stability Perspective"**

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration for publication in the *International Journal of Emerging Markets* (IJOEM). The manuscript presents an empirical study of the export intensity–performance relationship across two waves of World Bank Enterprise Survey data for Chinese private manufacturing firms (2012 and 2024), with a focus on the stability of nonlinear turning points and the moderating roles of technological capability and digital adoption.

**Relevance to IJOEM**

The study contributes directly to IJOEM's core interest in firm-level dynamics in emerging markets. China's private manufacturing sector represents one of the largest and most institutionally complex emerging-market contexts for I–P research, and the cross-wave design (2012 and 2024) allows the study to test whether the curvilinear I–P relationship is structurally durable or wave-specific across a decade of China's institutional and digital transition, a question of direct relevance to IJOEM's readership. The study also advances understanding of how foundational digital adoption and technological capability operate in China relative to less and more advanced emerging economies.

**Key Contributions**

First, a robust inverted-U relationship between export intensity and labour productivity is confirmed in both waves, with turning points clustered in a tight band (49.4% in 2012, 47.2% in 2024, 48.8% pooled) with overlapping confidence intervals. Paternoster (1998) tests do not reject coefficient equality across waves (FSTS z = +0.82, p = .412; FSTS² z = −0.61, p = .545), providing evidence for the structural durability of the coordination-cost mechanism (H2b) over an environmental-shift account (H2a) in a rapidly changing emerging-market context.

Second, technological capability (TCI) is robustly and positively associated with labour productivity in both waves (β_z = +0.28 in 2012, +0.43 in 2024; both p < .001), with returns that increase over the period, although TCI does not robustly moderate the I–P curvature. This is consistent with a resource-based reading in which the productivity value of technological capability rises as market institutions support more knowledge-intensive production.

Third, digital adoption (DAI), measured by a single-item digital-presence proxy, is positively associated with productivity but is retained as a baseline control rather than a capability construct, consistent with the Conditional Digital Capability Moderation (CDCM) framework's prediction that in economies where internet diffusion is widespread but foundational digital infrastructure remains heterogeneous, a selection effect dominates the causal productivity premium. This finding complements P3 Vietnam (IV-null DAI) and P4 Singapore (DAI as a high-export contingent resource), providing three-country triangulation of the CDCM framework.
