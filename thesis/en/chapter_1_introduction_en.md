## 1.1 Research Background

Firm internationalization has been one of the central themes of international-business research for more than half a century. Since Johanson and Vahlne (1977) proposed the Uppsala model of sequential internationalization, decades of scholarship have accumulated in an effort to answer a foundational question: does internationalization genuinely improve firm performance, and under what conditions does it do so? Lu and Beamish (2004) provided important evidence of an S-shaped (S-curve) relationship between the degree of internationalization and performance, while Contractor et al. (2003) and numerous subsequent studies have continued to challenge, qualify, and refine that conclusion. Although the scholarly debate remains unresolved, the present consensus holds that the internationalization–firm performance (I–P) relationship is real but complex, contingent on a range of boundary conditions.

In Asia, this context is especially worthy of attention because the region is witnessing markedly uneven rates of economic integration and digital transformation across countries. From Singapore — a small open economy that leads the world in innovation capacity and global connectivity — to Vietnam — a transition economy accelerating institutional reform and international integration — and then to China, with its vast market size and distinctive institutional architecture, and even to the small Pacific island economies confronting the dual existential threats of climate change and an export-dependency trap, this diversity creates an ideal field laboratory for testing the boundary conditions of the I–P relationship (Khanna & Palepu, 2010; Peng, 2003).

Digital transformation is fundamentally reshaping how firms internationalize and how internationalization affects performance. Banalieva and Dhanaraj (2019) argue that internalization theory must be updated for the digital economy, because digital data and platform-mediated interaction reduce the reliance on physical presence. Stallkamp and Schotter (2021) show that digital platforms internationalize according to a logic entirely different from that of traditional multinational corporations. Verhoef et al. (2021) offer a three-stage analytical framework ranging from digitization to digitalization and digital transformation. Nonetheless, most of the existing empirical evidence on the role of digital capability in the I–P relationship remains concentrated in developed economies — notably North America and Western Europe — while evidence for emerging Asia is still very limited (Bhandari et al., 2023).

The role of institutions in shaping internationalization outcomes cannot be overlooked. Institutional theory (North, 1990; Khanna & Palepu, 2010) holds that institutions generate differing incentive structures and transaction costs across countries. In emerging markets, "institutional voids" — the absence of the intermediary institutions that support market transactions — impose substantial additional costs on internationalizing firms (Peng, 2003). The institutional diversity across Asian economies — from economies with stable state governance and strong rule of law, such as Singapore, to economies with developing institutions, such as Cambodia or the Pacific island states — yields a particularly rich institutional gradient for testing. This dissertation approaches that gradient systematically through a six-group institutional classification developed for this study, abbreviated ICRV (Institutional Context Regime Variation).

Finally, top managers play an indispensable role in shaping internationalization strategy and outcomes. Upper-echelons theory (Hambrick & Mason, 1984) holds that strategic decisions reflect managers' cognition, experience, and values. International experience helps managers identify opportunities and reduce the costs of the liability of foreignness, while gender diversity in the leadership team can strengthen risk management and multidimensional decision-making (Hsu et al., 2013; Post & Byron, 2015). Incorporating the managerial tier into a multi-tier explanatory framework is necessary to capture fully the complexity of the I–P relationship in the Asian context.

## 1.2 Research Problem

Despite hundreds of empirical studies, the evidence on the relationship between internationalization and firm performance remains inconsistent and fragmented. Meta-analyses of the I–P relationship consistently report a pooled effect that is positive but small — a pooled correlation coefficient r in the range of 0.07–0.13 — together with very high between-study heterogeneity, with I² frequently exceeding 80% (Bausch & Krist, 2007; Kirca et al., 2012; Marano et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2022). This high heterogeneity is not a signal of methodological error; rather, it is evidence that the I–P relationship depends deeply on contextual conditions. Arte and Larimo (2022) argue that the high heterogeneity implies the need to shift from single-variable models toward a multi-tier framework that accounts for institutions, capabilities, and managerial characteristics.

Three important research gaps are identified from the literature review. The first gap is the absence of an integrative theoretical framework capable of explaining that high heterogeneity. Existing studies typically select one or two theories as their foundation, yielding models that are insufficiently multi-tier to capture real-world complexity. The Uppsala model explains learning costs but is inadequate for the role of institutions; the resource-based view (RBV) explains capability differences but neglects macro-context; institutional theory explains the macro-context but does not integrate individual managerial characteristics. No single theory standing alone can fully explain that heterogeneity. The second gap is the unwarranted conflation of two distinct digital constructs: technological capability — the depth of technological competence embedded in the organization — and digital adoption — the surface-level extent to which digital tools are applied in business operations. Many prior studies have combined these two constructs into a single composite index, which reduces the precision of conclusions and may obscure the differing mechanisms through which they operate (Bharadwaj et al., 2013; Coltman et al., 2008; Verhoef et al., 2021). The third gap is the lack of large-scale cross-country evidence for Asia. Most existing studies focus on a single country or sub-region, and even where multi-country studies exist, their scope is often narrow and lacks sufficient institutional diversity to test meaningful boundary conditions.

This dissertation is designed to address these three gaps by building and testing a multi-tier explanatory framework for the I–P relationship in Asia, using a mixed synthesis-empirical design that combines meta-analysis with large-scale multi-country analysis. The scientific focus of the dissertation is the moderating role of institutions, digital capability, and top-manager characteristics — three moderating tiers that, the dissertation argues, jointly constitute the explanatory structure required for the high heterogeneity in the I–P relationship.

## 1.3 Research Objectives

The general objective of the dissertation is to build and test a multi-tier explanatory framework for the relationship between internationalization and firm performance in Asian economies, clarifying the moderating role of institutions, digital capability, and top-manager characteristics.

From this general objective, the dissertation pursues four specific objectives. The first specific objective is to synthesize and update the meta-analytic evidence on the I–P relationship over the period 1977–2026, establishing the pooled effect, the level of heterogeneity, and the principal moderators that explain heterogeneity in the literature. The second specific objective is to test specific mechanisms across different national contexts, including Singapore as an advanced small open economy, Vietnam as an expanding transition economy, and China as a large emerging economy, in order to clarify how I–P mechanisms operate differently by national context. The third specific objective is to extend the analysis to 49 economies (including Japan) across the Asia-Pacific (including seven Pacific SIDS) to test the generalizability of the findings across a broad scope, inheriting and extending the 17-economy pool from component study P1. The fourth specific objective is to analyze the moderating role of three groups of variables: institutions (including the ICRV sub-regimes and indicators of institutional barriers), digital capability (clearly distinguishing the technological capability index — TCI — from the digital adoption index — DAI), and top-manager characteristics (experience and gender).

---

## 1.4 Research Questions

The central research question of the dissertation is: How does internationalization affect firm performance in Asia, and which conditions — relating to institutions, digital capability, and top-manager characteristics — explain the high heterogeneity in that relationship?

From this central question, four specific research questions are posed. The first research question (RQ1) is: What is the pooled effect of internationalization on firm performance in the 1977–2026 literature, what is the level of heterogeneity, and which moderators explain that heterogeneity systematically? The second research question (RQ2) is: Within specific national contexts including Singapore, Vietnam, and China, through which distinctive mechanisms does the I–P relationship operate, and how does the nonlinearity manifest? The third research question (RQ3) is: Across the multi-country Asian scope comprising 49 economies (including Japan) across the Asia-Pacific (including seven Pacific SIDS), does the moderating role of the digital adoption index (DAI) and the technological capability index (TCI) generalize, and do these two constructs genuinely operate through different mechanisms? The fourth research question (RQ4) is: What is the role of institutions — measured by the ICRV sub-regime — together with the distinction between TCI and DAI, and top-manager characteristics, in moderating the I–P relationship, and how significant is the three-way interaction among those three tiers?

## 1.5 Research Object and Scope

### 1.5.1 Research Object

The research object of the dissertation is the relationship between internationalization and firm performance (the internationalization–firm performance relationship) together with the factors that moderate that relationship, namely institutional context, digital capability, and top-manager characteristics. The unit of analysis is the firm at the organizational level (firm-level analysis), not the industry or country level.

### 1.5.2 Spatial Scope

The primary spatial scope of the study comprises **49 economies (including Japan)** across the Asia-Pacific with data in the World Bank Enterprise Surveys (WBES), classified and organized into six sub-regimes within the ICRV framework. The six sub-regimes are: (i) Advanced Innovation-Driven, represented by Singapore, South Korea, and Taiwan; (ii) Advanced Resource-Driven, represented by the GCC economies; (iii) Upper-middle, represented by China and Malaysia; (iv) Lower-middle transition, including Vietnam and the developing ASEAN economies; (v) Emerging, including Asia's least developed economies such as Cambodia, Nepal, and Afghanistan; and (vi) SIDS (Small Island Developing States), comprising seven small Pacific island economies (the main P8 sample; extended to nine economies including Comoros and Timor-Leste in robustness checks), included to test the extreme boundary conditions of the I–P relationship. Japan enters the empirical frame for the first time through the inaugural 2025 WBES wave (component study P10), placed within ICRV Group I (Advanced Innovation-Driven).

### 1.5.3 Temporal Scope

The temporal scope of the study has two levels. For the meta-analysis phase, the dissertation synthesizes the literature over the period 1977–2026, spanning the entire research program on the I–P relationship from the appearance of the first empirical studies to the completion of the dissertation. For the multi-country empirical analysis phase, the dissertation uses WBES data over the period 2006–2026, comprising 87,987 firms across 99 country-year pairs (including Japan 2025) spanning 14 survey waves. WBES data from three schema generations (PICS3 over 2009–2012, Standardized over 2013–2017, and BREADY/BEE over 2018–2025) were harmonized to ensure consistency in variable measurement across survey waves.

## 1.6 Methodology and Research Methods

The dissertation applies a mixed synthesis-empirical research design — a combination of meta-analysis and multi-country empirical analysis — to answer the four research questions posed. This design is not merely the parallel use of two methods; it creates a two-way dialogue: the meta-analysis provides a systematic overview of the state of the evidence and identifies the important moderators in the literature, while the empirical analysis directly tests the hypotheses arising from the theoretical framework in the Asian context. This interaction substantially strengthens the contribution relative to using a single method alone (Borenstein et al., 2009; Hunter & Schmidt, 2004).

As for the specific methods, the meta-analysis phase uses a random-effects model with Fisher's z transformation, subgroup analysis by institutional regime, and publication-bias tests including Egger's test and the Begg–Mazumdar rank-correlation test. The empirical phase uses OLS regression with robust HC1/HC3 standard errors, quadratic and cubic models to test nonlinearity, two- and three-way interactions to test moderation, together with country and year fixed effects. The Lind–Mehlum U-test is applied to confirm the inverted-U form and to locate the turning point in the nonlinear relationship (Haans et al., 2016; Lind & Mehlum, 2010).

## 1.7 Scientific Hypotheses (Summary)

Building on the integrative multi-tier theoretical framework comprising the Uppsala model, RBV, institutional theory, upper-echelons theory, and the digital-capability lens, the dissertation proposes a set of scientific hypotheses with six core hypotheses. The baseline relationship between internationalization and firm performance takes a nonlinear form — specifically an inverted-U — with high entry and adaptation costs in the early stage and benefits of scale, learning, and market diversification in the later stage, until coordination costs exceed benefits and lead to a decline in performance. The technological capability index (TCI) positively moderates the I–P relationship by widening the adaptation margin and strengthening the capacity to exploit competitive advantages in international markets. The digital adoption index (DAI) — understood as a proxy for foundational digital adoption — has a non-monotonic moderating effect contingent on the institutional regime: in economies with strong institutions, DAI amplifies the benefits of internationalization; in economies with weak institutions, DAI can amplify coordination costs once the degree of internationalization exceeds a threshold. Institutional context, measured by the ICRV sub-regime, moderates both the form and the magnitude of the I–P relationship, generating a gradient from a large positive effect in Advanced Innovation-Driven economies to a negative effect in the Emerging and SIDS extreme. Top-manager characteristics — specifically international experience and gender diversity — positively moderate the I–P relationship through mechanisms that strengthen opportunity recognition and risk management. Beyond the six core hypotheses H1–H6, the dissertation states an additional boundary-condition hypothesis H1b: in SIDS economies (ICRV Group VI), where three minimal structural conditions are simultaneously violated — a domestic market that is too small, extremely high trade costs, and an absence of institutional support — the I–P relationship shifts from an inverted-U form to a **monotonically negative relationship with no turning point**, termed the Forced Internationalization Penalty (FIP). The full set of hypotheses H1–H6 and H1b is presented in detail in Chapter 2 of the dissertation.

## 1.8 Scientific and Practical Significance

### 1.8.1 Theoretical Contributions

Theoretically, the dissertation contributes to the international-business literature along several principal lines. The first line is the integration of four foundational theories — the Uppsala model, the resource-based view, institutional theory, and upper-echelons theory — into a unified multi-tier explanatory framework, supplemented by the digital-capability lens to reflect contemporary digital transformation. This framework is not a mechanical juxtaposition of theories but a theoretical architecture with internal consistency, in which each tier explains a different type of heterogeneity in the I–P relationship. The second line is the clear separation of technological capability (TCI) and the digital adoption index (DAI) as two independent theoretical constructs, grounded in different conceptual foundations from Bharadwaj et al. (2013), Verhoef et al. (2021), and Coltman et al. (2008). This separation addresses an important conceptual gap in the literature on digitalization and internationalization. The third line is the repositioning of the Uppsala model in the AI era by extending the learning mechanism to encompass "data-augmented learning," consistent with the trend of digitally facilitated internationalization (Banalieva & Dhanaraj, 2019; Yang et al., 2025). The fourth line is the proposal and theoretical naming of the **Forced Internationalization Penalty (FIP)** — a novel theoretical construct not previously named in the international-business literature — as the extreme boundary condition of the inverted-U model when all three tiers of the CDCM framework (institutions, capabilities, governance) are simultaneously at their minimum. Empirical evidence from the seven Pacific SIDS confirms the monotonically negative relationship with no turning point, opening a new research direction on the boundary conditions of internationalization theory.

### 1.8.2 Methodological Contributions

Methodologically, the dissertation contributes along two lines. The first line is the mixed synthesis-empirical design combining meta-analysis and multi-country analysis, which makes it possible to triangulate between the synthesized evidence from the global literature and the empirical evidence from the Asian context. The second line is the development and application of the ICRV institutional classification framework for 49 economies (including Japan), providing a new classification tool with potential application in many future studies on the moderating effect of institutions in the international-business literature.

### 1.8.3 Practical Contributions

In practical terms, the dissertation provides a systematic basis for two principal groups of users. For firms, the dissertation's analytical results show that there exists an optimal threshold in the relationship between the degree of exporting and performance, and that this threshold varies with each firm's institutional context and digital capability. Managers must recognize this threshold and invest strategically in technological capability rather than focusing solely on surface-level digital adoption. For policymakers, the evidence from the dissertation suggests that institutional reform and support for digital transformation can have a positive complementary effect — termed the "digital shield effect" by the study of 17 emerging Asian economies — whereby digital capability mitigates the adverse impact of institutional barriers on internationalizing firms (component study P1). This mechanism is tested by the dissertation across a broader scope of 49 economies (including Japan) across the Asia-Pacific.

## 1.9 Novelty of the Study

The dissertation has six points of novelty relative to prior work. The first point of novelty is the proposal of an integrative multi-tier framework for the I–P relationship in the Asian context, combining four foundational theories with the digital-capability lens into a unified and testable theoretical architecture. No prior study in the Asian context has systematically built an integrative framework that fully incorporates these three moderating tiers. The second point of novelty is the separation of TCI and the digital adoption index (DAI) as two non-overlapping constructs, testing them separately within the same model to clarify the differing mechanisms of these two types of digital capability. This separation remedies a limitation of prior studies that typically pool together two constructs of differing nature. The third point of novelty is the development and application of the ICRV institutional classification framework with six sub-regimes for 49 economies (including Japan), including the seven adjacent Pacific SIDS as the extreme boundary case; this is the first time a classification system of such granularity has been applied in empirical research on the I–P relationship in Asia. The fourth point of novelty is the largest data pool to date for multi-country research on the I–P relationship in Asia, with 87,987 firms from 49 economies (including Japan) across the Asia-Pacific, substantially extending the 17-economy pool (component study P1) that was the largest existing foundation prior to the completion of the dissertation. The fifth point of novelty is testing the temporal stability of the turning point in the I–P relationship in China, allowing an assessment of whether that nonlinear structure is stable across more than a decade of digital transformation. The dissertation's analytical results show that the turning point is relatively stable across two observation points, providing evidence of the durability of the nonlinear I–P structure in China despite substantial changes in the economic context. The sixth point of novelty is the discovery and theoretical naming of the **Forced Internationalization Penalty (FIP)** in the seven Pacific SIDS — for the first time in the international-business literature, a case of a monotonically negative I–P relationship with no turning point is documented and theoretically justified by three structural conditions violated simultaneously, opening a new research direction on the boundary conditions of universal internationalization theories.

## 1.10 Structure of the Dissertation

The dissertation is organized into five chapters according to the traditional doctoral-dissertation standard of the School of Economics, Can Tho University. Chapter 1 — Introduction — sets out the research problem, identifies the gaps, and presents the objectives, research questions, scope, methodology, a summary of the scientific hypotheses, the significance, the novelty, and the structure of the dissertation. Chapter 2 — Theoretical Foundations and Literature Review — builds the integrative multi-tier theoretical framework, reviews the existing empirical evidence, and develops the hypotheses H1–H6 based on the gaps and the theoretical logic. Chapter 3 — Research Methods — presents in detail the research design, the data sources, the variable measurement, the analytical models, and the robustness-testing strategy. Chapter 4 — Research Results — presents the results at three levels of analysis: the updated meta-analysis for 1977–2026, the country-specific contextual analysis, and the capstone multi-country analysis on the full pool of 49 economies (including Japan). Chapter 5 — Discussion and Conclusion — integrates the findings from Chapter 4 into the theoretical framework of Chapter 2, discusses the scientific contributions, the managerial and policy implications, the limitations of the study, and the directions for future research.

**Roadmap of the component studies (P3–P9 detailed below; the full programme comprises 11 component papers, P1–P11):** The dissertation integrates eleven component papers (P1–P11) published in or under review at ISI/Scopus journals; the empirical core P3–P9 is detailed below, with P10 (Japan, inaugural 2025 WBES wave) and P11 (JED digital-divide) extending the programme. **P3** (Vietnam — JABS, under review): tests the inverted-U in ICRV Group IV, turning point = 39.3–46.2% FSTS, with TCI a significant level-shifter. **P4** (Singapore — MIR, under review): tested in ICRV Group I, turning point ≈ 88.6% (with a very wide confidence interval exceeding the feasible range of FSTS, hence not reliably located — see Section 4.3), with DAI Tier-1+2 amplifying the I–P curve. **P5** (China — IJOEM, under review): tests temporal stability, with the turning point stable across 2012–2024 (≈ 48.78%, Paternoster z = 0.82, p = .412). **P6** (three-level meta-analysis — IBR, under review): k = 238 studies, r̄ = 0.074, I² = 62.4%, ICRV gradient Q_M = 17.35 (df = 4, p = .002). **P7** (49 economies including Japan — JIBS, under revision): main regression sample N = 80,373 (M2; N = 78,445 in the full model M5), turning point 43.5% in the full model M5 (51.5% in the unconditioned quadratic M2), with DAI entering as a significant level-shifter (+0.20, p < .001) whose curve-reshaping interactions are not significant in the 49-economy pool (reshaping appears only in single-economy contexts such as Singapore, P4); the first integrated CDCM test at large cross-country scale. **P8** (SIDS — World Development, under revision): the FIP boundary condition, a monotonically negative relationship (FSTS β = −1.339, p < .001), confirming H1b in the seven Pacific SIDS. **P9** (India — MIR/IJOEM, under review): the durability of the I–P threshold across three WBES waves 2014–2025 (~29,000 firms), with the discovery of the **dissolution** of the inverted-U turning point under extreme institutional turbulence (TP 61.8% to 40.7% to dissolved; Paternoster z = −7.94, p < .0001), accompanied by a negative DAI Tier-2/UPI interaction. The results P3–P9 are presented in detail in Chapter 4 and integrated into the CDCM discussion in Chapter 5.
