**Submission of Manuscript:** "Institutional Context, Digital Adoption, and the Internationalization–Performance Relationship: A Three-Level Meta-Analysis"

Dear Editors-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration as a Research Article in *Management International Review*. The paper reports a pre-registered, three-level meta-analytic regression analysis (MARA) of the internationalization–performance (I-P) relationship across a geographically diverse corpus of *k* = 238 studies (*K* = 288 effect sizes) from 49 economies, and documents a field-level finding about the integrity of the accumulated I-P evidence base in international management research. The analysed corpus is self-contained and definitive for the article's conclusions; a parallel pre-registered WoS + Scopus expansion (Path B) is disclosed for transparency but is treated as a prospective replication corpus rather than as a condition on the current findings.

**Fit with Management International Review.** MIR's editorial mission centres on rigorous theoretical and empirical contributions to international management — including meta-analytic syntheses that consolidate, critically appraise, and reframe IB evidence. The I-P relationship is the most meta-analyzed question in IB research, yet pooled positive effects coexist with substantial unresolved heterogeneity. Our contribution is methodological and corrective: we apply a three-level model with full PRISMA 2020 reporting and OSF pre-registration, decompose within- versus between-study variance, and quantify how much of the literature's apparent signal survives a formal publication-bias correction. The findings speak directly to MIR's IB scholarly readership about what four decades of I-P evidence warrant as a basis for theory development and managerial inference.

**Key contributions.**

First, this is the first three-level MARA applied to the I-P literature. Decomposing total heterogeneity (*I*² = 87.8%) shows that the dominant share is *within*-study (Level-2 *I*² = 76.1%) rather than *between*-study (Level-3 *I*² = 11.8%). This evidence reframes how the field should interpret I-P heterogeneity: effect-size dispersion is driven more by within-paper design variation (measurement, sample-frame, model specification) than by between-country contextual differences, with direct implications for how IB scholars should model and report I-P estimates going forward.

Second, this is the first pre-registered meta-analytic test of three theoretically grounded but previously untested moderators: institutional context regime (ICRV), country-level digital adoption (cDAI), and Digital Paradox Lifecycle (DPL) phase. The hypothesized contingencies are largely *not* supported: the ICRV between-regime test is statistically significant in the full sample (*Q*_M = 17.35, *df* = 4, *p* = .002) but is fragile to a drop-Frontier sensitivity analysis (*Q*_M = 1.49, *p* = .68); cDAI (*Q*_M = 1.23, *p* = .541) and DPL phase (*Q*_M = 0.56, *p* = .755) are non-significant. These transparent null results bound the conditions under which country-level digital and institutional contingencies could plausibly operate at the meta-analytic level — a contribution that, in our view, is more valuable than yet another set of confirmatory positive moderator effects.

Third — and most consequential for MIR's IB readership — the analysis documents substantial publication bias. Trim-and-fill imputes *k* = 58 missing studies and cuts the pooled effect from *r* = .074 to *r* = .035, an implied $\approx$ 53% attenuation (Begg *p* < .001; Egger *p* = .057). The interpretation is that four decades of I-P evidence may be materially inflated by publication selection. For IB theory: residual heterogeneity in the I-P literature plausibly reflects publication-side selection more than the substantive contextual moderators (institutional development, digital readiness, governance quality) commonly invoked. For managerial inference: the bias-corrected average performance return to internationalization is meaningfully smaller than the published evidence base suggests.
