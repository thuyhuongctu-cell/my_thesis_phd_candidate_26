**Submission of Manuscript:** "Institutional Context, Digital Adoption, and the Internationalization–Performance Relationship: A Three-Level Meta-Analysis"

Dear Professor Newburry,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration as a Research Paper in *Multinational Business Review*. The paper reports a pre-registered, three-level meta-analytic regression analysis (MARA) of the internationalization–performance (I-P) relationship across a globally representative corpus of *k* = 238 studies (*K* = 288 effect sizes) from 49 economies, and surfaces a field-level finding about the integrity of the accumulated I-P evidence base in multinational business research.

**Fit with Multinational Business Review.** MBR's mandate is to advance "innovative theory and practical insights on the multinational firm." The I-P relationship is the most meta-analyzed question in international business, yet pooled positive effects coexist with substantial unresolved heterogeneity. Our contribution is methodological and corrective: we apply a three-level model with full PRISMA 2020 reporting and OSF pre-registration, and we quantify how much of the literature's apparent signal survives a formal publication-bias correction. The findings speak directly to MBR's MNE-management readership about what the four-decade I-P evidence base does and does not warrant as a basis for strategic decisions.

**Key contributions.**

First, this is the first three-level MARA applied to the I-P literature. Decomposing total heterogeneity (*I*² = 87.8%) shows that the dominant share is *within*-study (Level-2 *I*² = 76.1%) rather than *between*-study (Level-3 *I*² = 11.8%) — evidence that effect-size dispersion is driven more by within-paper design variation than by between-country contextual differences, which reframes how the field should interpret I-P heterogeneity in MNE research.

Second, this is the first pre-registered meta-analytic test of three theoretically grounded but previously untested moderators: institutional context regime (ICRV), country-level digital adoption (cDAI), and Digital Paradox Lifecycle (DPL) phase. The hypothesized contingencies are largely *not* supported: the ICRV between-regime test is statistically significant (*Q*_M = 17.35, *df* = 4, *p* = .002) but is fragile to a drop-Frontier sensitivity analysis (*Q*_M = 1.49, *p* = .68), while cDAI (*Q*_M = 1.23, *p* = .541) and DPL phase (*Q*_M = 0.56, *p* = .755) are non-significant. These transparent null results bound the conditions under which country-level digital and institutional contingencies could plausibly operate at the meta-analytic level.

Third — and most consequential for MBR's MNE-management readership — the analysis documents substantial publication bias. Trim-and-fill imputes *k* = 58 missing studies and cuts the pooled effect from *r* = .074 to *r* = .035, an implied ~53% attenuation (Begg *p* < .001; Egger *p* = .057). This indicates that four decades of I-P evidence may be materially inflated by publication selection. The practical implication for MNE managers and policy makers: the average performance return to internationalization is meaningfully smaller than commonly cited estimates suggest, and firm-level digital capability — not country-level digital infrastructure alone — appears more decisive for translating internationalization into performance.
