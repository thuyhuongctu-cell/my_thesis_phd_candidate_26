**Submission of Manuscript:** "Institutional Context, Digital Adoption, and the Internationalization–Performance Relationship: A Three-Level Meta-Analysis"

Dear Professor Peng,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration as a Research Paper in the *Asia Pacific Journal of Management*. The paper reports a pre-registered, three-level meta-analytic regression analysis (MARA) of the internationalization–performance (I–P) relationship across a *k* = 238 / *K* = 288 corpus drawn from 45 economies and four decades (1977–2025), and contributes a new firm-relevant institutional contingency construct — the **Institutional Context Regime Variation (ICRV)** taxonomy — to the Asia-Pacific IB literature.

**Fit with the Asia Pacific Journal of Management.** APJM's mandate is to advance management research that is theoretically and empirically grounded in the Asia-Pacific region and that speaks to firm strategy in heterogeneous institutional contexts. The I–P relationship is the most meta-analyzed question in international business, yet pooled positive effects coexist with substantial unresolved heterogeneity (*I*² regularly exceeds 80% across prior meta-analyses), and existing meta-analytic typologies have not disaggregated the Asia-Pacific institutional spectrum at the resolution required to test theoretical predictions about regime-contingent capability deployment. Our contribution is exactly that disaggregation: we develop and formally test, at the meta-analytic level, an institutional contingency construct (ICRV) that orders 45 Asian and Pacific economies on a two-axis institutional-capability × resource-vulnerability ordering, and we integrate established channels from prior IB theory — rent protection (Kogut & Zander, 1993), liability-of-foreignness attenuation (Zaheer, 1995; Peng, Wang, & Jiang, 2008), and institutional-void amplification (Khanna & Palepu, 2010) — into a single mechanism that predicts the signatures of an institutional gradient in cross-border capability deployment. The findings speak directly to APJM's mandate to advance theory and evidence on firm strategy under heterogeneous institutional regimes in the Asia-Pacific region.

**Key contributions.**

First, this is the first **three-level meta-analytic test of an integrated capability–institution mechanism** in the Asia-Pacific I–P literature. The mechanism, developed in §2.2, synthesises three established sub-channels through which home-country institutions condition the productivity returns to internationalisation: (i) **rent protection** (strong IP and contract enforcement preserve proprietary rents across foreign markets; Kogut & Zander, 1993; Zaheer, 1995); (ii) **liability-of-foreignness attenuation** (transparent regulation shrinks the discriminatory and informational costs of cross-border operation; Peng, Wang, & Jiang, 2008; Zaheer, 1995); (iii) **institutional-void amplification** (weak institutions force firms to invest in substitute governance — relationship capital, political connections, informal contracts — that depress net returns; Khanna & Palepu, 2010). The mechanism is tested through three pre-registered hypotheses: H1 (ICRV between-regime variance), H2 (DPL phase moderation), and H3 (country-level digital adoption cDAI moderation).

Second, this is the first three-level MARA applied to the I–P literature. Decomposing total heterogeneity (*I*² = 87.8%) shows that the dominant share is *within*-study (Level-2 *I*² = 76.1%) rather than *between*-study (Level-3 *I*² = 11.8%) — evidence that effect-size dispersion is driven more by within-paper design variation than by between-country contextual differences. This reframes how the field should interpret I–P heterogeneity in Asia-Pacific firm research.

Third, the ICRV institutional gradient receives **fragile support**: the full-sample between-regime Q_M test is significant (*Q*_M = 17.35, *df* = 4, *p* = .002) but collapses to non-significance under a drop-Frontier sensitivity test (*Q*_M = 1.49, *p* = .68), indicating that current sample sizes lack sufficient between-regime variation in the diagnostic cells (particularly Frontier *K* = 3 and Upper-middle *K* = 25) to fully adjudicate the gradient. The directional propositions E1a and E1b and the digital-adoption moderators (cDAI Q_M = 1.23, *p* = .541; DPL Q_M = 0.56, *p* = .755) are similarly not robustly confirmed at *k* = 238. These transparent null results bound the conditions under which country-level institutional and digital contingencies could plausibly operate at the meta-analytic level, and they set up a rigorous, pre-registered formal-search replication on a corpus of *k* ≈ 600–700 (Path B, deposited on OSF) as the natural next step.

Fourth, and most consequential for APJM's Asia-Pacific firm-management readership, the analysis documents **substantial publication bias** in the four-decade I–P evidence base. Trim-and-fill imputes *k* = 58 missing studies and cuts the pooled effect from *r* = 0.074 to *r* = 0.035, an implied ~53% attenuation (Begg *p* < .001; Egger *p* = .057). The practical implication for Asia-Pacific managers and policy makers is that the average performance return to internationalisation in the regional literature may be materially smaller than commonly cited estimates suggest, and firm-level capability investment is likely to dominate country-level digital infrastructure as a determinant of cross-border performance returns.
