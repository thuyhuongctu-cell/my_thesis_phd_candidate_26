**Submission of Manuscript: "Revisiting the Internationalisation–Performance Relationship in an Emerging Market: The Roles of Technological Capability and Foundational Digital Adoption"**

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration for publication in the *Journal of Economics and Development* (JED). The manuscript presents an empirical study of the internationalisation–performance (I–P) relationship using three waves of World Bank Enterprise Survey (WBES) data for Vietnam (2009, 2015, 2023; N = 2,958).

**Fit with JED**

JED is an exceptionally appropriate venue for this work. The journal promotes scholarship that improves understanding of economics and development with a particular focus on developing and transition economies, and it welcomes applied, empirical, and comparative studies at the micro level. Our study sits squarely within this scope: it provides firm-level (micro) evidence on how a lower-middle-income transition economy converts export participation into productivity, and on the institutional and capability conditions under which that conversion succeeds or fails. Vietnam — a transition economy whose institutional trajectory spans WTO accession (2007), CPTPP ratification (2019), and the post-COVID digital-economy push (2023) — is precisely the kind of development context JED prioritises, and the findings carry direct, transferable implications for industrial and digital-economy policy across comparable developing economies.

**Key Contributions**

First, the study provides multi-wave within-country evidence that the inverted-U relationship between export intensity and labour productivity is structurally durable: turning points cluster at 39–46% of direct-export intensity across all three waves (Lind–Mehlum test p ≤ .013 per wave; p < .001 pooled), a narrow band sustained through fourteen years of major institutional change. We show this durability is most informative as a boundary-condition finding: single-country institutional contexts preserve functional-form regularity that cross-national pooling tends to obscure.

Second, the study is the first systematic separation of technological capability (TCI: quality certification, foreign-licensed technology) from foundational digital adoption (DAI: website presence, Tier-1 only) as non-overlapping constructs in a WBES-based I–P study. Technological capability is robustly positive across all waves; the website-based digital indicator follows a trajectory consistent with Tier-1 proxy obsolescence (positive in 2009, null in 2015, negatively interactive with export intensity by 2023). Instrumental-variable estimation attributes the pooled digital-adoption association to selection rather than to productive capacity.

Third, Paternoster cross-wave z-tests confirm that the 2009–2015 decline in the digital-adoption productivity association (z = 3.353, p < .001) and its 2015–2023 partial recovery (z = −2.051, p = .040) are statistically distinguishable shifts — a pattern with direct implications for the design of digital-economy programmes in transition economies.

**Development and policy relevance.** Consistent with JED's mission, the paper translates these results into concrete guidance: the institutionally embedded turning-point band (39–46% FSTS) reframes the policy question from "does internationalisation help?" to "under what institutional and capability conditions does the productive threshold shift?" — and identifies technological-capability upgrading, rather than surface digital presence alone, as the lever that moves it.
