**Submission of Manuscript: "Revisiting the Internationalisation–Performance Relationship in an Emerging Market: The Roles of Technological Capability and Foundational Digital Adoption"**

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration for publication in the *Journal of Economics and Development* (JED). The paper presents an empirical study of the internationalisation–performance (I–P) relationship using three waves of World Bank Enterprise Survey data for Vietnam (2009, 2015, 2023; N = 2,958), directly aligned with JED's mandate to advance scholarly research on economics and development in transition economies with a particular focus on developing-country contexts.

**Fit with JED**

JED is a natural venue for this work for three reasons. First, the journal's editorial mission explicitly prioritises sustainability-oriented economics and development research in developing and transition economies — exactly the analytical lens of the present study. Vietnam serves not as a parochial case but as a theoretically informative setting in which to test boundary conditions on the digital-internationalisation and institutional-voids literatures, with direct relevance to JED's readership of development economists, policy makers, and management scholars working on emerging Asia. Second, the three-wave design (spanning WTO accession in 2007, CPTPP ratification in 2019, and the post-COVID digital-economy push in 2023) lets us trace how an institutional trajectory reshapes a capability–performance link over fourteen years — the kind of long-horizon, policy-relevant evidence that aligns with JED's stated interest in evaluating economic-policy episodes and drawing lessons for Vietnam's sustainable-growth agenda. Third, the paper engages the cross-national I–P debate (e.g., Wu, Fan, & Chen, 2022, *Management International Review*) to show that within-country institutional context preserves functional-form regularity that pooled cross-national estimates obscure — a methodological contribution to JED's growing portfolio on Vietnamese firm-performance research.

**Key Contributions**

First, the study provides multi-wave within-country evidence that the apparent inverted-U between export intensity and labour productivity is structurally durable in standard FSTS-and-FSTS² specifications: turning points cluster at 39–46% across all three waves (Lind–Mehlum test p ≤ .013 per wave; p < .001 pooled), a 7-percentage-point band sustained through 14 years of major institutional change. Critically, decomposition shows this is best read as a *step function across the export-participation barrier* — when refit on the exporter-only subsample the quadratic FSTS term collapses to non-significance (β = −0.200, p = .660) — consistent with the well-established trade-and-selection mechanisms of Melitz (2003), Wagner (2007), and Bernard et al. (2007, 2012). Our contribution is to document that in three Vietnamese WBES cross-sections, the participation-margin effect is large enough to explain the *entire* apparent inverted-U; we therefore qualify rather than overturn the cross-national skepticism of Pisani, Garcia-Bernardo, and Heemskerk (2020, *Strategic Management Journal*).

Second, the study is the first systematic separation of technological capability (TCI: quality certification, foreign-licensed technology) from foundational digital adoption (DAI: website presence, Tier-1 only) as non-overlapping primary constructs in a WBES-based I–P study. Technological capability is robustly positive across all waves (β = 0.123–0.215); the website-based digital indicator undergoes a trajectory consistent with Tier-1 proxy obsolescence (positive in 2009, null in 2015, negatively interactive with export intensity in 2023). Instrumental-variable estimation attributes the pooled DAI association to selection rather than productive capacity.

Third, Paternoster cross-wave z-tests confirm that the 2009–2015 fall in the digital-adoption productivity association (z = 3.353, p < .001) and its 2015–2023 partial recovery (z = −2.051, p = .040) are statistically distinguishable shifts — an empirical pattern that carries direct policy implications for digital-economy programme design in transitional Vietnamese contexts: Tier-1 digital indicators (website presence) saturate as basic organisational hygiene factors, so the next phase of Vietnam's national digital-transformation agenda must shift policy instruments toward Tier-2 and Tier-3 capabilities (e-commerce platform integration, ERP adoption, AI-augmented operations) if measurable productivity gains are to be realised at the firm level.

**Policy Relevance for Vietnam**

The findings speak directly to two of Vietnam's current economic-policy priorities. (i) Export-promotion design: the 39–46% turning-point band identifies the bounded operating range within which Vietnamese SMEs can sustain export expansion without productivity erosion — a benchmark for current MOIT (Ministry of Industry and Trade) export-support programmes. (ii) Digital-transformation policy: the Tier-1 saturation finding indicates that the current Decision 749/QĐ-TTg (National Digital Transformation Programme to 2030) website-presence indicators have become uninformative as policy targets, and that the National Digital Economy Strategy needs to refocus on higher-tier digital capability metrics to remain diagnostically useful. These implications are developed in §5.3 of the manuscript.
