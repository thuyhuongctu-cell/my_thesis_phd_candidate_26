## A.2 Endogeneity, selection, and PSM (Panels E, J, K)

Three approaches address selection and endogeneity concerns. A note on the selection structure precedes the checks. Exporters self-select into foreign markets, so OLS estimates reflect both selection and treatment effects. As a boundary condition, exporter participation declined from 28.4% (2009) to 18.8% (2023), consistent with servicification and the gradual exit of marginal exporters, which itself attenuates a constant positive-selection story across waves. A Heckman two-stage check confirms this reading: Heckman two-step corrections (Panel E) yield insignificant inverse-Mills ratios across all waves (|λ| < 0.84, p > .25 in each wave), indicating no detectable selection bias on the exporter subsample. The Mills ratio coefficient does not materially change FSTS estimates across waves, suggesting limited selection bias in this context and that the participation-margin productivity gap documented in §4.4 reflects a genuine performance discontinuity rather than positive selection.

Propensity-score matching (Panel J) corroborates the average TCI association and the baseline DAI_z level association without imposing linearity: ATT estimates for foreign-technology / certification status (0.609–0.637, p < .001) and for website ownership (0.298–0.321, p < .001) are substantively consistent with OLS, though the latter is best read as a descriptive Tier-1 selection-corrected association rather than a capability test. Full matching-balance diagnostics and ATT estimates appear in Table A1 (J rows).

Two-stage least squares using leave-one-out sector × region × wave peer-adoption rates as instruments (Panel K; first-stage F = 22–35, well above the Staiger–Stock threshold) returns an instrumented DAI estimate of 0.018 (p = .942) and an instrumented TCI estimate of 1.639 (p < .001). The null 2SLS DAI coefficient is a key result: website presence does not plausibly cause productivity improvements in 2023 Vietnam, reinforcing the Tier-1 proxy obsolescence interpretation of the negative DAI × FSTS interaction. The strongly positive instrumented TCI (β = 1.639, p < .001, larger than OLS, consistent with attenuation-bias correction) confirms that foreign-technology and standards capability is the robust productivity-relevant mechanism. The instrument set satisfies relevance (strong first stage) and approximate exclusion (peer-adoption rates are unlikely to affect individual firm productivity through channels other than DAI adoption, conditional on sector-wave cells). Oster (2019) δ-stability bounds (R_max = 1.3 × R_controlled) indicate no focal coefficient changes sign or collapses to zero under plausible unobserved selection.

## A.3 Measurement sensitivity (Panels A, B, D, F)

Core inferences do not depend on composite construction. Enriching TCI with R&D and product-innovation items (Panel A) attenuates the TCI coefficient modestly but does not alter qualitative conclusions: TCI_full_z = 0.096 (p = .024) in 2023, with M3 joint p = .152 (TCI moderation null on the broader composite). The enriched DAI_rich composite (Tier 1–2, Panel B) yields directionally consistent moderation in 2023 with weaker significance (continuous-form M8 joint p = .099; binary-form M8 joint p = .243). Micro-firm exclusion (Panel D) preserves the inverted-U and TCI associations (FSTSc = +0.794, p = .008; FSTSc² = -1.647, p < .001; TCI_z = +0.188, p < .001).

## A.4 Sector and sample-composition (Panels G, H, I)

**Sector split (Panel G).** Re-estimating M2 / M7 / M8 separately on manufacturing firms (sector1 ∈ {1, 2, 3}, ISIC 15–37, N = 1,854) and non-manufacturing firms (utilities, construction, wholesale and retail, transport, finance and other services; sector1 ∈ {4, 5, 6, 7}; N = 1,104) preserves the inverted-U in both subsets but sharper in manufacturing (FSTSc β = 0.971, p = .001; FSTSc² β = -1.883, p < .001) than non-manufacturing (FSTSc β = 1.615, p = .064; FSTSc² β = -2.479, p = .046). The capability and digital-adoption channels operate primarily in manufacturing: M7 yields TCI_z β = 0.223 (p < .001) and DAI_z β = 0.087 (p = .009) in manufacturing vs only marginal TCI_z (β = 0.090, p = .096) and null DAI_z (β = 0.068, p = .133) in non-manufacturing. The DAI moderation channel concentrates in manufacturing (M8 joint p = .103, marginal; FSTSc × DAI_z = -0.543, p = .079) and is uniformly null in non-manufacturing (M8 joint p = .280). TCI moderation, by contrast, is distinguishable from zero in both subsets (M3 joint p = .011 manufacturing; p = .007 non-manufacturing), indicating that the curvature-flattening effect of capability operates broadly while the digital channel remains specific to the manufacturing exporter base. This is consistent with the view that basic digital adoption interacts with cross-border production-coordination demands more strongly in tradable-goods sectors than in services or domestically oriented sectors.

**Exporter-only sub-sample (Panel H).** Because exporter share is 28.4%, 20.7% and 18.8% across waves, the inverted-U on the full sample is partly identified by the participation margin between FSTS = 0 and FSTS > 0. Re-fitting M2 / M7 / M8 on the exporter-only sub-sample (N = 281, 198, 190 in 2009, 2015, 2023; 669 pooled) yields a negative linear FSTSc term (β = -0.861, p < .001) but non-significant quadratic (FSTSc² β = -0.200, p = .660); the joint M8 curvature-plus-moderation block is not significant (joint F p = .462). Wave-specific exporter-only estimates are noisier and weaker. The reading: the inverted-U in the main specification is meaningfully identified by the participation margin rather than purely within-exporter intensity variation, consistent with §3.1 descriptive evidence that direct-export intensity is zero-inflated in Vietnamese WBES data, with limited mass between the participation margin and the implied turning point. The substantive H1 claim is best read as a non-monotonic association between participation-and-intensity in exporting and productivity rather than a strict within-exporter intensity-curvature claim.

**Pooled wave × focal interaction test (Panel I).** Re-estimating pooled M8 with a saturated set of wave interactions on focal terms (FSTSc × wave, FSTSc² × wave, DAI_z × wave, TCI_z × wave) indicates that only DAI_z × wave is statistically distinguishable from the pooled average (joint p = .016); FSTSc, FSTSc² and TCI_z direct-effect cross-wave differences are not separable (all joint p > .25); FSTSc × DAI_z and FSTSc² × DAI_z cross-wave differences are likewise not separable (joint p > .55). This formal test is consistent with the descriptive Paternoster result: only DAI direct shifts are cross-wave-distinguishable, while curvature parameters and the FSTS × DAI moderation terms share a common pooled magnitude across waves.

## A.5 Functional form and density (cubic extension; ±5 pp band density)

**Cubic extension.** Augmenting pooled M2 with a cubic FSTSc³ term yields a non-significant cubic coefficient (β = -1.763, p = .287); fit criteria favour the quadratic (ΔAIC = -0.99; ΔBIC = -7.0 when moving from cubic to quadratic). The cubic term does not alter the turning-point estimate or any focal coefficient substantively. The simpler inverted-U (M2) is preferred on both inferential and parsimony grounds.

**Density around the turning point.** Defining a ±5 percentage-point band around the wave-specific TP, the share of firms within the band is 0.6% (6 of 989) in 2009, 1.2% (11 of 956) in 2015, 0.9% (9 of 1,013) in 2023, and 1.0% (29 of 2,958) pooled. The bulk of mass lies at FSTS = 0; turning-point estimates are identified primarily through the contrast between non-exporters and exporters and the right tail above the TP, not through dense within-band variation. Readers should bear this density structure in mind when interpreting the precise turning-point magnitude.

## A.6 Multiple-testing caveat

§4.5 reports four narrative panels (G, H, I, F) and three robustness families (endogeneity/selection, measurement sensitivity, sample composition) across multiple focal terms. We do not apply a formal multiple-testing correction because the panels probe different identification concerns rather than testing the same hypothesis repeatedly, but readers should weight any single marginal panel result accordingly. The substantive inferences in §5 rely on the pattern across panels and the directional consistency of focal estimates rather than on the significance of any single panel.
