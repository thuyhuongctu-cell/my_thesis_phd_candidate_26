**Submission of Manuscript: "Technological Capability, Digital Adoption, and the Internationalisation–Performance Relationship: A Firm-Level Study of Singapore"**

Dear Editor-in-Chief,

We are pleased to submit the above manuscript for consideration for publication in the *Journal of Asian Business and Economic Studies* (JABES). The paper presents an empirical study of the internationalisation–performance (I-P) relationship using World Bank Enterprise Survey microdata for Singapore (2023; N = 623; N = 617 for specifications including DAI), positioned as a boundary-case test at the advanced-economy ceiling of the Asian institutional spectrum.

**Fit with JABES**

JABES is a natural venue for this work for three reasons aligned with the journal's editorial scope. First, JABES's mandate to publish scholarly research on **Asian business, management, and economic studies** matches the paper's analytical focus: Singapore — Asia's most digitally mature economy — provides a within-Asia boundary-case complement to the cross-Asian I-P evidence portfolio (companion studies on Vietnam, China, and Pacific SIDS firm-level data, all anchored in WBES microdata across the Asian institutional spectrum). Second, JABES's Q1 Scopus positioning and EconLit / ERIM / ASEAN Citation Index multi-indexing reach an audience of Asian business economists and IB scholars for whom the digital-saturation paradox documented here is a directly actionable theoretical refinement. Third, JABES's editorial preference for rigorous theoretical and empirical contributions aligned with the Asian institutional context fits the paper's primary positioning: rather than a parochial single-country report, Singapore is treated as the analytically informative high-institution case study of *internationalisation-to-scale*, which complements the *internationalisation-to-survive* regime documented in companion work on emerging Asia.

**Key Contributions**

First, within the observed export-intensity range, the I-P relationship in Singapore is better characterised as predominantly positive with mild quadratic curvature than as a formally identified inverted-U. The fitted quadratic implies a turning point near $\mathrm{FSTS} \approx 82\%$, but that point falls in a sparsely populated region of the data (only $\approx$3% of firms exceed 50% FSTS) — consistent with theoretical expectations that advanced-economy digital infrastructure attenuates the coordination-cost mechanism underlying the classic inverted-U until very high levels of export intensity. This qualifies rather than overturns the conventional nonlinear I-P literature for digitally mature Asian economies.

Second, the study documents the empirical signature of the **digital saturation paradox** at the advanced-economy ceiling: in an environment where Tier 1–2 digital interfaces (websites, electronic payment systems) are already widely diffused, foundational digital adoption functions as a hygiene factor for domestic operations rather than a differentiating asset. The diffusion-driven saturation mechanism is well established (Rogers, 1962; Bharadwaj et al., 2013); our contribution is to document its specific empirical signature in WBES Singapore 2023, where DAI's productivity association becomes measurable primarily in the high-export tail ($\beta_5$ for $\mathrm{FSTS}^2 \times \mathrm{DAI}$ = +3.119, p = .005), consistent with digital adoption operating as a contingent scaling resource rather than a uniform productivity premium.

Third, the study provides the clearest empirical separation to date between the Technological Capability Index (TCI: quality certification, R&D, product innovation, foreign-licensed technology) and the Digital Adoption Index (DAI: website presence, electronic payment) as non-overlapping constructs in a WBES-based I-P study — a design feature that directly operationalises the Conditional Digital Capability Moderation (CDCM) framework and resolves construct-bundling ambiguities common in the digital-IB literature.

**Policy Relevance for Asian Advanced Economies**

The findings speak directly to Singapore's Smart Nation 2.0 (2024) and Enterprise Singapore export-promotion programmes, and have natural extensions for other digitally mature Asian economies (Hong Kong, Taiwan, Korea, Japan): foundational digital adoption alone is insufficient to generate broad-based productivity gains in digitally saturated economies; export-intensity orientation is the channel through which Tier 1–2 digital investments translate into measurable productivity uplift. The implication for Asian export-promotion policy is to treat foundational digital adoption as an export-readiness condition rather than a productivity-premium asset, and to prioritise Tier 3–4 capabilities (ERP integration, AI-enabled operations) for productivity uplift across the firm population.
